## ABSTRACT

**Keywords:** Phase-field method; Hydro-mechanical coupling; Hydraulic fracture; Dynamic; COMSOL Multiphysics

This paper presents a comprehensive study on phase-field modelling in COMSOL Multiphysics for simulating dynamic hydraulic fracturing in porous media based on Biot's poro-elasticity theory. The focus is on addressing the challenges associated with crack width estimation in this context. A new strain-based crack width formulation is proposed, offering improved accuracy in predicting fracture permeability and ease of implementation in numerical approaches. The model's capabilities are extended to consider dynamic crack propagation by incorporating the kinetic energy in the governing coupled hydro-mechanical-damage equations. The numerical implementations in COMSOL Multiphysics are thoroughly explained, providing insights into the techniques used to solve the governing equations. Verification examples, including the benchmark KGD verification, are presented to demonstrate the model's capabilities in simulating hydraulic fractures in porous media and validate its accuracy and reliability. A final numerical example focusing on the dynamics of crack propagation in a gravity dam is simulated, allowing for a comprehensive examination of the model's performance. The proposed strain-based crack width formulation and consideration of dynamic crack propagation contribute to improved accuracy in predicting fracture permeability.

## 1. Introduction

The interrelation of fluid flow and deformation in fractured porous media is a crucial area of research in energy and environmental modelling, particularly in regards to hydraulic fracturing, often referred to as "fracking". This technique involves injecting a high-pressure fracking fluid into a well-bore to create cracks in the deep porous media. This process aims at optimising hydrocarbon production and enhancing the overall efficiency of geothermal energy piles [1–3]. The proliferation of fracking projects is steadily increasing owing to their considerable economic advantages. However, this practice encounters opposition due to its potential environmental ramifications, including concerns over groundwater contamination, air quality risks, and chemical migration [4,5]. This scrutiny highlights the need for reliable mathematical and computational models to effectively address fluid flow in fractures surrounded by a poro-elastic medium.

Although analytical solutions of hydraulic fracturing based on linear elastic fracture and fluid mechanics [6–11] are available, they are limited to relatively straightforward 2D problems featuring a single crack. Hence, numerical simulations provide the benefit of being able to model complex geometries and loading cases [12]. Over the years, a variety of numerical models have been proposed to simulate this process, including, but not limited to the phase-field method [13–21], XFEM (extended finite element method) [22–27], and meshfree methods [28,29]. To address the intricate characteristics of hydraulic fracturing, numerical methods are unquestionably the most promising and cost-effective tools.

Phase-field modelling of hydraulic fracture emerged as a very promising approach due to the capability of easily handling complex fracture geometries and also capturing fracture propagation without requiring an explicit representation of the fracture geometry or topology. For an extensive review of the pertinent literature, the reader is referred to Y. Heidar [30]. Following the principles of Griffith [31], the most commonly employed phase-field models have been established by Bourdin et al. [32], Miehe et al. [33], and Wu [34]. They incorporate various degradation and energy dissipation functions each with their own benefits. Having compared the previously mentioned models [35], Chen and de Borst recommend the phase-field model initially introduced by Miehe et al. [15], which is based on a generalised variational approach for a Darcy–Biot-type bulk response of hydro-poro-elasticity [33].

While many existing models focus on static or quasi-static crack propagation, the importance of dynamic crack propagation lies in its direct relevance to incorporating the dynamic effects in many critical engineering applications involving high-speed crack propagation. A review of quasi-static and dynamic models of brittle fracture can be found in [36]. Hofacker and Miehe present a robust formulation for dynamic brittle crack propagation [37] as an extension to [33]. While extensive research has been conducted concerning the phase-field modelling of dynamic brittle fracture [37–41], there remains a significant gap in studies considering the simultaneous influence of hydro-mechanical coupling and dynamic effects. Only a limited number of works have explored this area, with Zhou et al. being one of the few researchers having developed a model for fluid-driven dynamic crack propagation in porous media [42]. However, the accurate determination of crack width values remains a significant obstacle in phase-field methods, leaving room for further improvement.

One of the main challenges in phase-field modelling of a porous medium is the accurate estimation of crack width [18]. The phase-field variable represents the fracture region as a diffuse interface, which makes it difficult to obtain crack width values directly. This challenge is particularly relevant for practical applications of hydraulic fracturing, as the crack width plays a crucial role in determining the value of the hydro-fracture permeability. Most studies have considered a constant permeability for the fracture. While, other researchers have addressed the estimation of crack aperture in hydraulic fracturing by proposing various integration approaches [18,43,44]. Alternative approaches have been suggested, encompassing formulations reliant on the nominal strain [16,45] or solely on the phase-field parameter [21]. As a more precise approach, Lee et al. [46] introduced a level-set method. This technique calculates the crack aperture based on the phase-field value and the displacement field. However, these methods often suffer from inaccuracies or difficulties in their implementation within numerical frameworks or commercial software. Despite these advancements, the need for a comprehensive crack width formulation for phase-field modelling remains a challenge. Addressing this gap, developing a robust framework that encompasses all aspects of dynamic hydro-fracturing in a porous medium is a significant advantage. In this regard, COMSOL Multiphysics stands out as a versatile commercial software package that offers a comprehensive platform for implementing multi-phase examples, featuring efficient built-in solvers [47].

It is noteworthy to highlight that XFEM has been successfully implemented in COMSOL Multiphysics, as documented in studies by Jafari et al. [48,49]. Although there have been limited research efforts on implementing the phase-field method in COMSOL Multiphysics, with notable works by Zhuang et al. [50] and Zhou et al. [42], none of these studies have addressed and incorporated the effects of variable crack width in dynamic hydraulic fracturing.

This paper aims to provide a comprehensive study of phase-field modelling for hydraulic fracturing in porous media, based on Biot's poro-elasticity theory, with a particular emphasis on the challenges associated with estimating crack width and inertial effects. A new strain-based crack width formulation is proposed, offering improved accuracy in predicting the fracture permeability, while also providing ease of implementation in numerical approaches. The capabilities of the model are extended to consider dynamic crack propagation by incorporating the kinetic energy in the governing coupled hydro-mechanical-damage equations. The numerical implementation of the proposed approach in COMSOL Multiphysics is fully explained in this paper. Verification examples, such as the benchmark KGD verification, are presented to demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed model in simulating hydraulic fractures in porous media. As a final numerical example, a gravity dam is simulated to thoroughly examine the application of the model in the dynamics of crack propagation.

## 2. Methodology

The phase-field model is a popular smeared approach for simulating crack propagation, including hydraulic fracturing in porous media. This section describes the phase-field equations for fracture propagation, the model's assumptions, and the COMSOL Multiphysics implementation steps to solve the phase-field equations.

### 2.1. Phase-field governing equations

Assuming an arbitrary body of $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n (n \in \{1,2,3\})$ as the reference configuration of a cracked material, having an external boundary $\partial\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n-1}$ with an internal fracture denoted as $\Gamma \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n-1}$. The corresponding total energy functional, $\Psi$, for the entire domain can be decomposed into

$$
\Psi = \Psi^s + \Psi^d + \Psi^p - \Psi^k - \Psi^e, \tag{1}
$$

where $\Psi^s$, $\Psi^d$, $\Psi^p$, $\Psi^k$, and $\Psi^e$ represent the total stored elastic energy, the dissipated fracture energy, the hydraulic pressure energy, the kinetic energy, and the external work, respectively. In the following sections, we present the formulation and variation of each term.

#### 2.1.1. Elastic energy: $\Psi^s$

For an isotropic homogeneous linear elastic material, the elastic energy can be calculated based on the formulation presented by Miehe et al. [33] as

$$
\Psi^s = \int_{\Omega} \psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \phi) \, dV, \tag{2}
$$

in which $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}(\mathbf{u})$, the linear strain tensor, and $\psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon})$, the energy per unit of volume, can be expressed as

$$
\boldsymbol{\epsilon} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \nabla \mathbf{u} + \nabla \mathbf{u}^T \right), \tag{3}
$$

and

$$
\psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) = \frac{\lambda}{2} \langle \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \rangle^2 + \mu \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^2), \tag{4}
$$

where $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are Lamé constants.

The tensile energy is the key term that causes fracture growth [33], hence, the phase-field is only associated with the positive part of the elastic energy. To consider that, the positive (tensile) and negative (compressive) parts of the elastic energy, denoted as $\psi^{s+}$ and $\psi^{s-}$ can, respectively, be expressed as:

$$
\psi^{s+}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) = \frac{\lambda}{2} \langle \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \rangle_{+}^2 + \mu \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{+}^2), \tag{5}
$$

and

$$
\psi^{s-}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) = \frac{\lambda}{2} \langle \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \rangle_{-}^2 + \mu \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{-}^2). \tag{6}
$$

Here, $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{+}$ and $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{-}$ are obtained by decomposing the strain tensor into its tensile and compressive parts, respectively. It is assumed that the phase-field only affects the positive part of the elastic energy. Therefore, to account for the stiffness reduction of fractured material, a degradation function $g(\phi)$ is introduced and multiplied by $\psi^{s+}$, resulting in

$$
\psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \phi) = g(\phi) \cdot \psi^{s+}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) + \psi^{s-}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}). \tag{7}
$$

To properly distinguish between the fractured and undamaged zone, the degradation function $g(\phi)$ must satisfy certain conditions. Specifically, it must satisfy $g(0)=1$ to represent the undamaged state, $g(1)=0$ to represent the completely damaged state, and $g'(1)=0$ to ensure that the damaged region has zero energy dissipation. Following the work of Miehe et al. [33], the degradation function can be defined as follows:

$$
g(\phi) = \left[ (1-k)(1-\phi)^2 + k \right]. \tag{8}
$$

Here, a value of $1\times10^{-9}$ is considered in all simulations.

The variation of the total elastic energy can be calculated as:

$$
\delta \Psi^s = \int_{\Omega} \left[ \frac{\partial \psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\phi)}{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} : \delta \boldsymbol{\epsilon} + \frac{\partial \psi^s(\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\phi)}{\partial \phi} \delta \phi \right] dV, \tag{9}
$$

and based on Eqs. (7) and (8), the variation can be rewritten as:

$$
\delta \Psi^s = \int_{\Omega} \left[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}' : \nabla \delta \mathbf{u} - 2(1-k)(1-\phi)\psi^{s+} \delta \phi \right] dV. \tag{10}
$$

In this equation, $\boldsymbol{\sigma}'$ is the effective stress tensor which, based on Eqs. (5) to (7), can be written as:

$$
\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = g(\phi) \left[ \lambda \langle \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \rangle_{+} \mathbf{I} + 2\mu \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{+} \right] + \left[ \lambda \langle \operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \rangle_{-} \mathbf{I} + 2\mu \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{-} \right]. \tag{11}
$$

#### 2.1.2. Fracture energy: $\Psi^d$

In Eq. (1), $\Psi^d$ represents the dissipated fracture energy, which is defined by the smeared crack energy dissipation functional:

$$
\Psi^d = \int_{\Gamma} G_c \, dS, \tag{12}
$$

where $G_c$ denotes the energy release rate caused by crack evolution/propagation. Utilising the crack surface density per unit volume $\gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi)$, Eq. (12) can be rewritten as:

$$
\Psi^d = \int_{\Omega} G_c \, \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) \, dV, \tag{13}
$$

where the crack surface density is defined based on [33] as:

$$
\gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) = \frac{\phi^2}{2l_0} + \frac{l_0}{2} |\nabla \phi|^2. \tag{14}
$$

The variation of the dissipated fracture energy with respect to the phase-field parameter $\phi$ is given as:

$$
\delta \Psi^d = \int_{\Omega} G_c \, \delta \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) \, dV, \tag{15}
$$

where the variation of the crack surface density with respect to the damage field $\delta \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi)$ can be obtained by:

$$
\delta \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) = \frac{\phi}{l_0} \delta \phi + l_0 \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla \delta \phi. \tag{16}
$$

#### 2.1.3. Hydraulic pressure energy: $\Psi^p$

Following the work of Lee et al. [51], the fluid driving energy can be obtained from:

$$
\Psi^p = -\int_{\Omega} \alpha p \, (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) \, dV. \tag{17}
$$

Here, $p$ represents the pore fluid pressure and $\alpha = 1 - (K_T / K_s)$ is Biot's coefficient. $K_s$ and $K_T$ are the bulk moduli of the solid grains and the entire porous medium, respectively. The variation of the hydraulic pressure energy $\Psi^p$ with respect to the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ is given by:

$$
\delta \Psi^p = -\int_{\Omega} (\alpha p \mathbf{I}) \, \delta \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \, dV, \tag{18}
$$

where $\mathbf{I}$ denotes the second-order identity tensor.

#### 2.1.4. Kinetic energy: $\Psi^k$

In order to accurately model the phenomenon of dynamic fracture, it is crucial to ensure that the energy functional being used in the model incorporates both the potential and kinetic energy components. Specifically, the functional must encompass the kinetic energy term $\Psi^k$ as:

$$
\Psi^k = \int_{\Omega} \frac{1}{2} \left( \rho \dot{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{u}} \right) dV, \tag{19}
$$

in which $\rho$ is the density of the mixture and $\dot{\mathbf{u}}$ is the derivative of the displacement field with respect to time which defines the velocity field. The variation of the kinetic energy is given as:

$$
\delta \Psi^k = \int_{\Omega} \rho \ddot{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV, \tag{20}
$$

where $\ddot{\mathbf{u}}$ represents the second derivative of the displacement field with respect to time.

#### 2.1.5. External work: $\Psi^e$

The work done by external forces including a distributed body force per unit mass $\mathbf{b}^*$ and prescribed traction on the boundary $\mathbf{t}^*$ of the system can be expressed as:

$$
\Psi^e = \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{b}^* \cdot \mathbf{u} \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega_t} \mathbf{t}^* \cdot \mathbf{u} \, dS. \tag{21}
$$

The variation of the external work can be readily calculated as:

$$
\delta \Psi^e = \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{b}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega_t} \mathbf{t}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dS. \tag{22}
$$

By minimising the total energy functional, we can derive the equations that describe the behaviour of the system in terms of the displacement and crack phase-field. Considering Eqs. (10), (15), (18), (20), and (22), the variation of the total energy in Eq. (1) leads to:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\delta \Psi = & \int_{\Omega} \left( \boldsymbol{\sigma}' : \nabla \delta \mathbf{u} - 2(1-k)(1-\phi)\psi^{s+} \delta \phi \right) dV + \int_{\Omega} G_c \, \delta \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) \, dV \\
& - \int_{\Omega} (\alpha p \mathbf{I} : \nabla \delta \mathbf{u}) \, dV - \int_{\Omega} \rho \ddot{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV - \left( \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{b}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega_t} \mathbf{t}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dS \right).
\end{aligned} \tag{23}
$$

The weak form of the governing equations for the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ and crack phase-field $\phi$ can be obtained by minimising the total energy functional. For this purpose, first, Eq. (23) is set to zero $\delta \Psi = 0$ with respect to the two field variables, $\mathbf{u}$ and $\phi$:

$$
\int_{\Omega} (\boldsymbol{\sigma}' - \alpha p \mathbf{I}) : \nabla \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV - \int_{\Omega} \rho \ddot{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV - \left( \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{b}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega_t} \mathbf{t}^* \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dS \right) = 0, \tag{24a}
$$

$$
\int_{\Omega} G_c \left[ \frac{\phi}{l_0} \delta \phi + l_0 \nabla \phi \cdot \nabla \delta \phi \right] dV - \int_{\Omega} 2(1-k)(1-\phi) \psi^{s+} \delta \phi \, dV = 0. \tag{24b}
$$

By employing the (Gauss) divergence theorem and the chain rule, the weak form yields:

$$
\int_{\Omega} (\nabla \boldsymbol{\sigma} + \mathbf{b}^* - \rho \ddot{\mathbf{u}}) \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega_t} (\mathbf{t}^* - \boldsymbol{\sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n}) \cdot \delta \mathbf{u} \, dS = 0, \tag{25a}
$$

$$
\int_{\Omega} \left[ G_c \left( \frac{\phi}{l_0} - l_0 \nabla^2 \phi \right) - 2(1-k)(1-\phi) \psi^{s+} \right] \delta \phi \, dV + \int_{\partial \Omega} G_c (l_0 \nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{n}) \cdot \delta \phi \, dS = 0, \tag{25b}
$$

where $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is the Cauchy stress tensor given as $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \boldsymbol{\sigma}' - \alpha p \mathbf{I}$. Considering the variations of the displacement field $\delta \mathbf{u}$ and the damage field $\delta \phi$ in the weak form — see Eq. (25), the strong form of the governing equations can be obtained as:

$$
\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma} - \rho \ddot{\mathbf{u}} + \rho \mathbf{b}^* = 0, \tag{26a}
$$

$$
\left[ \frac{2 l_0}{G_c} (1-k) \psi^{s+} + 1 \right] \phi - l_0^2 \nabla^2 \phi = \frac{2 l_0}{G_c} (1-k) \psi^{s+}. \tag{26b}
$$

With the Neumann boundary conditions given below:

$$
\boldsymbol{\sigma} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{t}^* \quad \text{on } \partial \Omega_t, \tag{27a}
$$

$$
\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial \Omega. \tag{27b}
$$

It should be highlighted that the sign convention of continuum mechanics is adopted throughout with tension taken as positive and compression as negative. Pore water pressure is considered positive, following the geomechanics convention.

### 2.2. Fluid pressure governing equations

Following the work of Miehe and Mauthe [15], the Darcy-Poiseuille law is used to model the fluid flow in both fractures and porous media. This approach allows for a unified description of fluid flow, simplifying the analysis of fluid transport in heterogeneous materials. The linear momentum balance equation of the pore fluid, in conjunction with the continuity equation, are utilised in the u-p formulation to describe fluid flow within saturated porous media [52]. Assuming that the relative acceleration of the fluid compared to the acceleration of the solid phase is negligible, the generalised Darcy equation is expressed as:

$$
\nabla \cdot \dot{\mathbf{w}} + \alpha \nabla \cdot \dot{\mathbf{u}} + \frac{1}{Q} \dot{p} = 0, \tag{28}
$$

where the velocity vector of the solid skeleton is denoted by $\dot{\mathbf{u}}$, while the relative velocity vector of the fluid phase with respect to the solid phase is represented by $\dot{\mathbf{w}}$. The compressibility coefficient $Q$ is defined as:

$$
\frac{1}{Q} = \frac{n}{K_f} + \frac{\alpha - n}{K_s}. \tag{29}
$$

Here, $n$ denotes the porosity of the soil mass, and $K_s$, and $K_f$ are the bulk moduli of the solid grains and the fluid phase, respectively.

Using Darcy's law, which is based on the linear momentum balance equation for the fluid phase, the relative velocity vector $\dot{\mathbf{w}}$ is expressed as [53]:

$$
\dot{\mathbf{w}} = \frac{k_f}{\mu_f} \left( -\nabla p + \rho_f \mathbf{b}^* - \rho_f \ddot{\mathbf{u}} \right), \tag{30}
$$

where $k_f$ is the intrinsic permeability of the pore fluid, $\rho_f$ represents the fluid density, and $\mu_f$ denotes the dynamic viscosity of the fluid phase.

Aside from the boundary condition given in Eq. (27), the exterior boundary is additionally subjected to the boundary conditions given below

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathbf{u} = \bar{\mathbf{u}} \quad \text{on } \partial \Omega_u, \\
& p = \bar{p} \quad \text{on } \partial \Omega_p, \\
& \dot{\mathbf{w}} \cdot \mathbf{n}_r = \bar{q} \quad \text{on } \partial \Omega_w,
\end{aligned} \tag{31}
$$

where $\bar{\mathbf{u}}$ denotes the prescribed displacement on $\partial \Omega_u$ in which $\partial \Omega_u \cup \partial \Omega_t = \partial \Omega$ and $\partial \Omega_u \cap \partial \Omega_t = \emptyset$. In addition, $\bar{p}$ and $\bar{q}$ are the prescribed fluid pressure and the outflow or inflow fluid rate on the boundaries $\partial \Omega_p$ and $\partial \Omega_w$, respectively, which satisfy $\partial \Omega_p \cup \partial \Omega_w = \partial \Omega$ and $\partial \Omega_p \cap \partial \Omega_w = \emptyset$.

In order to model the fluid flow inside the fracture, the porosity, as well as Biot's coefficient $\alpha$, is set to unity where the solid is completely damaged using the following two equations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \alpha = \chi_R \times \alpha_0 + \chi_F, \tag{32a} \\
& n = \chi_R \times n_0 + \chi_F. \tag{32b}
\end{aligned}
$$

Here, $\alpha_0$ and $n_0$ are the initial Biot's coefficient and the porous material's porosity. $\chi_R$ and $\chi_F$ are linear indicator functions to decompose the domain into the reservoir and the fracture zone, respectively. Following the work of Lee et al. [51], the functions can be expressed as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \chi_F = \left( \frac{\phi - c_1}{c_2 - c_1} \right) H(\phi - c_1) H(-\phi + c_2) + H(\phi - c_2), \tag{33a} \\
& \chi_R = \left( \frac{c_2 - \phi}{c_2 - c_1} \right) H(\phi - c_1) H(-\phi + c_2) + H(-\phi + c_1), \tag{33b}
\end{aligned}
$$

where $H(x)$ stands for the Heaviside step function, $c_1$ and $c_2$ are values in the range of $(0,1)$, while $\phi > c_2$ specifies the fractured or damaged zone, and $\phi < c_1$ specifies the properties of the intact zone. $\phi$ values in between maximum and minimum values indicate the transition zone from the fracture to the intact domain.

Based on Oda [54], the equivalent permeability of the hydro-fracture in Eq. (30) is assumed to be isotropic and estimated using the cubic law as

$$
\mathbf{k}_{eq} = \left( k_f + \frac{1}{\kappa} \frac{w^3}{12 h_c} \right) \mathbf{I}, \tag{34}
$$

where the coefficient $\kappa$ is utilised to account for the assumption that the fracture surfaces are perfectly flat plates. Its value varies within the range of 1.04 to 1.65 based on the deviation from the ideal condition. In addition, $w$ denotes the crack opening and $h_c$ is the element size.

Since the phase-field method models fractures as a diffuse interface, rather than a sharp crack, it is a challenge to uniquely and more importantly accurately define the fracture surface. This can result in significant uncertainties in the calculation of fracture aperture. Moreover, the computation of the fracture aperture can also be computationally demanding. Despite these challenges, determining its value is a crucial step for a rigorous analysis of fractures in materials. The present study proposes a formula for estimating the crack width/opening based on strain analysis. The premise is that the material stiffness is reduced within the fracture, resulting in changes in the strain distribution within the elements, which is indicative of changes in the opening. The proposed strain-based formula is as follows and provides a good estimate of the crack opening:

$$
w = h_e \, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n \cdot \mathbf{n}_f. \tag{35}
$$

Here, $h_e$ and $\mathbf{n}_f$ denote the element size at the fracture domain, and the normal unit vector of the fracture, respectively. The normal strain vector, $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n$, is defined as the vector comprising the diagonal elements of the displacement gradient matrix, $\nabla \mathbf{u}$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n = (\epsilon_x, \epsilon_y, \epsilon_z)$ comprising the normal strains in the $x$-direction, $y$-direction, and $z$-direction. The orientation of $\mathbf{n}_f$, as outlined in Eq. (36), aligns with the gradient of the phase-field variable, denoted by $\nabla \phi$ [44,46]. However, since the damaged elements maintain a relatively constant phase-field variable $\phi$ of 1, the gradient $\nabla \phi$ vanishes over these elements. Despite some remedies proposed in the literature (see [44,46]), we have introduced an alternative approach to calculate the unit vector value. This approach is more compatible with built-in features of COMSOL. Accordingly, the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}_f$ within the fractured elements, over which $\nabla \phi$ is almost zero, can be obtained based on the normal strain vector $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n$ as described in Eq. (37). This equation incorporates the normal displacements caused by the fracture, accounting for the altered geometry, which properly calculates the unit vector within the fractured region. For more information and illustration refer to Appendix.

$$
\mathbf{n}_f = \frac{\nabla \phi}{|\nabla \phi|} \quad \text{for } \phi < 1, \tag{36}
$$

$$
\mathbf{n}_f = \frac{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n}{|\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n|} \quad \text{for } \phi = 1. \tag{37}
$$

The crack width representation can be simplified for either horizontal or vertical crack growth eliminating the need to calculate the normal vector, as discussed in more detail in Appendix. The simplified formulation incorporates the volumetric strain, $\epsilon_{\mathrm{vol}}$ as:

$$
w = h_e \, \epsilon_{\mathrm{vol}}. \tag{38}
$$

## 3. COMSOL Multiphysics implementation

### 3.1. Overview

COMSOL Multiphysics is a versatile simulation software designed for tackling multi-field problems. It utilises the finite element method as its underlying computational approach. Within this software, it is possible to integrate multiple physics phenomena by utilising the pre-existing built-in interfaces or by implementing custom user-defined physics modules [47]. By employing the appropriate physics interfaces available in COMSOL Multiphysics, it becomes possible to simulate the phase-field behaviour in a porous medium. The hydro-mechanical coupling of fluid flow within the porous medium is accomplished through the utilisation of two distinct physics interfaces. The first interface is the Solid Mechanics module, which employs general formulations of continuum mechanics to analyse the solid bulk. It also incorporates a library of suitable constitutive material laws. The second interface is the Darcy Flow module, which provides the necessary formulations for modelling low-velocity fluid flow through a porous medium. This interface covers a wide range of scenarios typically encountered in geomechanics. The Helmholtz equation and the domain Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) and Differential Algebraic Equations (DAEs) interface are the remaining critical interfaces necessary for the phase-field simulation. The selection of the Helmholtz equation is based on its close resemblance to the governing equations of phase-field. On the other hand, the distributed ODE node serves as a module that ensures the irreversible nature of the cracks. The complete process of the implementation is exhibited in Algorithm 1.

### 3.2. Solid mechanics

To satisfy the first governing equation in Eq. (26), the Solid Mechanics interface is employed considering that the inertial terms are included in the equation to ensure the correct simulation of dynamic crack propagation. In this module, there are various built-in material models to simulate the solid phase. For this purpose, the Linear Elastic Material node is incorporated. Adopting the Newton-Raphson approach and employing the weak forms in Eq. (25), the fourth order elasticity matrix, $\mathbf{D}$, needs to be adjusted at each time step based on the new strains. Hence, using Eq. (11), the elasticity matrix is written as:

$$
\mathbf{D} = \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}'}{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \lambda \left[ g(\phi) H(\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon})) + H(-\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol{\epsilon})) \right] \mathbf{J} + 2\mu \left[ g(\phi) \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_+}{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} + \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_-}{\partial \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \right], \tag{39}
$$

where the components of the matrix $\mathbf{J}$ are defined as $J_{ijkl} = \delta_{ij} \delta_{kl}$. Here, $\delta_{ij}$ and $\delta_{kl}$ are Kronecker deltas.

Once the material model has been established, the inclusion of gravitational forces as body forces can be achieved by utilising the Gravity node. Subsequently, a crucial step involves establishing a coupling between the solid phase and the fluid phase. To facilitate this coupling, the Solid Mechanics module must account for the influence of the pore water pressure on the stress values according to the definition of the effective stress. Consequently, the fluid pressure needs to be integrated into this interface as an external force, ensuring that the governing equation employs the concept of effective stress.

### 3.3. Darcy flow

Darcy's law interface serves as the foundation for simulating the fluid flow in porous media. Within this module, the selection of the Porous Medium node offers a comprehensive set of interfaces for defining both the properties of the fluid material and the porous matrix. In order to incorporate the governing equation mentioned in Eq. (28), the storage model is utilised to define the compressibility coefficient according to Eq. (29). In addition to the storage model modification, the equivalent permeability of the model is also modified according to Eq. (34). By updating the equivalent permeability in each time step, the model ensures the permeability of hydraulic cracks is adjusted according to the crack opening along the fracture. In order to facilitate hydro-mechanical coupling, the Mass Source node is incorporated into the system. This addition introduces a mass term on the right-hand side of Darcy's law, thereby accounting for the influence of the second term in Eq. (28), ensuring an accurate representation of the coupled hydro-mechanical behaviour within the system.

### 3.4. Distributed ODE and DAEs

In order to effectively handle the irreversible nature of cracks, it becomes crucial to update the positive part of the potential energy during each time step. This can be accomplished by comparing the positive potential energy value from the previous time step with the current value. Subsequently, at every time step, the maximum value between the two is selected as the positive part of the potential energy.

In addition, in order to generate an initial fracture inside the domain acting as an initial notch or natural fractures in the geometry, an initial value for the positive part of the potential energy, $\psi_0^+(\mathbf{x})$, can be considered.

$$
\psi_0^+(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases}
\dfrac{B G_c}{2 l_0} \left[ 1 - \dfrac{2 d(\mathbf{x},l)}{l_0} \right], & d(\mathbf{x},l) \leq \dfrac{l_0}{2} \\[10pt]
0, & d(\mathbf{x},l) > \dfrac{l_0}{2}
\end{cases} \tag{40}
$$

in which $d(\mathbf{x},l)$ is the distance of the nodes in location $\mathbf{x}$ from the centre line of the crack at location $l$. Considering $B = 1\times10^3$ ensures a phase-field value of $\phi = 1$ inside the predefined crack in the geometry, while satisfying the governing Eq. (26b).

### 3.5. Helmholtz equation

Considering that the governing equation — Eq. (26b) — resembles the Helmholtz equation, one can benefit from the built-in node in COMSOL Multiphysics of the specific PDE of Helmholtz. When defining the coefficients of this equation, the length scale value $l_0$, and the energy release rate must be incorporated. It is worth noting that the adjusted positive potential energy, previously calculated in each time step, is employed in the governing equation.

### 3.6. Analysis

In the final stage, the Time-Dependent Solver is employed when field variables change over time. The Segregated Node approach is utilised to handle segregated iterations within subsets. A sequence of three segregated steps is defined, and these steps are solved consecutively, utilising the updated results from the previous step as a basis for computation. In each time step, first, by solving the Eqs. (26a) and (28) in a fully-coupled manner, the displacement field and the pressure field are obtained. Next, the Previous Solution node is utilised to store the previous value of the positive potential energy. Taking into account all the prior values, the positive component of the potential energy is adjusted and computed. In the final segregated step, by employing Eq. (26b), the phase-field variable is solved. In the present study, the generalised-$\alpha$ method [55] is chosen as the time-stepping scheme.

## 4. Numerical simulations

This section provides numerical simulations of hydraulic fracturing in porous media to assess the performance of the proposed computational framework. It should be noted that due to the model's approach of minimising the energy functional to solve for the variables, it does not rely on the location of the initial crack or length being known. The simulations particularly emphasise the effect of incorporating the crack width formula in fracture permeability, in addition to investigating the influence of inertia in dynamic fracture problems. By addressing these factors, we aim to thoroughly understand how varying fracture permeability based on the fracture aperture affects the overall performance.

The first example involves a comparison between various crack width descriptions in the existing literature and the proposed crack width formula introduced in this research, see Eq. (35). By means of the second problem, the applicability of the model and the proposed crack width formula is verified using an analytical benchmark example that focuses on hydraulic fracturing in an infinite porous medium. The last example examines the dynamic characteristics of the proposed framework and its application in dam structures. This is achieved by conducting a comparative analysis of the results obtained from our framework with those reported in the existing literature using other numerical methods.

### 4.1. Crack width comparison

First, the proposed formulation to estimate the crack width is evaluated by comparing it to the existing methods in the literature. To do this, we analysed a 2D free plate that measures $4\,\text{m} \times 4\,\text{m}$, where a fracture of length $0.4\,\text{m}$ is introduced in the centre of the domain parallel to the $x$-axis. Setting the elastic modulus to $20\,\text{GPa}$, and assuming a Poisson's ratio of 0.2, tensile stress values equal to $100\,\text{kPa}$ are enforced on the upper and lower boundary. Fig. 2 shows the problem description and the boundary conditions. The crack mouth opening along the fracture is then obtained using different methods. The first method involves obtaining the discontinuous displacement field across the fracture from the finite element analysis of the domain and calculating the displacement jump accordingly [23]. The next method is based on a level-set approach proposed by S. Lee et al. [46] as shown in Eq. (41) where the crack width is calculated based on the displacement field and the level-set values based on the phase-field variable. Here, $C_{\mathrm{LS}}$ is a constant variable ranging between 0 and 1. A subsequent crack width interpolation inside the fracture is required. Finally, an integration-based approach, as investigated by Mikelic et al. [44], is considered. The formula representing this approach is detailed in Eq. (42). The previous methods are compared with the proposed method in this paper, and the results of the crack mouth opening along the fracture are demonstrated in Fig. 3.

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text{Level-set: } u = 2\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{n}_f = -2\mathbf{u} \cdot \frac{\nabla \phi_{\mathrm{LS}}}{\|\nabla \phi_{\mathrm{LS}}\|}, \quad \phi_{\mathrm{LS}} = \phi - C_{\mathrm{LS}}, \tag{41a} \\
& \text{Integration-based: } \text{COD} := w := w(x,y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \phi \, dy. \tag{41b}
\end{aligned}
$$

By discretising the domain with a mesh size of $h_e = 0.02\,\text{m}$, the results are shown in Fig. 3, where it is evident that the proposed method is in good agreement with the FEM results, while the other methods have a minimal error.

Next, the mesh dependency has been investigated by considering a discretisation using half, $h_e/2$, and twice the mesh size, $2h_e$, respectively. The mean squared errors are calculated for the data, resulting in values of $6\times10^{-8}$, $2.3\times10^{-8}$, and $2.07\times10^{-8}$ for $h_e = 0.04\,\text{m}$, $h_e = 0.02\,\text{m}$, and $h_e = 0.01\,\text{m}$, respectively. These simulations demonstrate the same level of accuracy with no considerable error, as depicted in Fig. 3.

The present technique has several advantages: Firstly, the calculation of Crack Mouth Opening Displacement (CMOD) using strain values is relatively straightforward, as opposed to the other methods involving higher calculation efforts, such as an interpolation of the width. It is evident in Eq. (41) that the level-set approach requires the assumption of a symmetric displacement at the fracture boundary, thereby introducing complexities to the calculation process. The additional calculation steps necessitate greater implementation efforts in COMSOL Multiphysics. On the other hand, the integration-based method is inherently incompatible with the built-in features of COMSOL Multiphysics for automation purposes. Another limitation is that the number of chosen lines is indicative of the attainable precision of the calculated crack width. Finally, the strain-based equation has an advantage in that the CMOD values are limited to the crack location, as shown in Fig. 4(b), whereas the level-set approach provides a CMOD contour for a more extensive area surrounding the crack, as demonstrated in Fig. 4(a).

## 5. Conclusions

This paper presents a phase-field model for simulating dynamic hydraulic fracturing in poroelastic media based on Biot's poroelasticity theory. The specific focus of this study is on addressing the challenges associated with crack width estimation. A novel strain-based crack width formulation is proposed, offering improved accuracy in predicting fracture permeability and ease of implementation in numerical approaches. The model is extended to consider dynamic crack propagation by incorporating kinetic energy into the governing coupled hydro-mechanical-damage equations. Moreover, the numerical implementations in COMSOL Multiphysics are thoroughly explained. The Open source implementation is made available on Github: https://github.com/sanashv/PFM-COMSOL.

Verification examples, including the benchmark KGD verification, are presented to demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed model in simulating hydraulic fractures in porous media. These examples showcase the accuracy and reliability of the model in capturing the essential features of hydraulic fractures. Finally, a more intricate numerical example is presented to investigate the application of the method in studying the dynamics of crack propagation. In this example, the hydraulic crack propagation is modelled in a gravity dam by considering various parameters and conditions, providing insights into the evolution of dynamic cracks in poroelastic media.

Overall, this paper contributes to the advancement of phase-field modelling in hydraulic fracture simulations, addressing key challenges associated with calculating fracture aperture, predicting fracture permeability, considering the inertial effects, and presenting an accurate and efficient approach for the analysis. The findings presented here have the potential to enhance our understanding and modelling capability of hydraulic fractures in porous media and facilitate the design and optimisation of related engineering processes.

## Appendix. Derivation of the crack width formulation

In this section, the motivation underlying the development of the crack width formula proposed in this research is discussed. In addition, a detailed description of the crack width formula is presented. The phase-field method possesses numerous advantages, including flexibility and robustness. This approach enables us to leverage the flexibility offered by smeared methods for solving complex fracture problems, while simultaneously ensuring an accurate representation of crack width — an aspect where smeared methods traditionally fell short compared to discrete methods. Hence, the proposed formula aims to improve its practicality by integrating strain values, and thus facilitating its implementation in various phase-field frameworks.

In phase-field modelling of hydraulic fracturing, the elements representing the fracture are considered fully damaged. Therefore, the strain resulting from the pressure inside the fracture is typically negligible compared to those corresponding to the opening value. Thus, it is supposed that the strain field is mainly attributed to the opening of the fracture. Fig. A.13a illustrates a simplified case of a crack oriented horizontally with a normal unit vector $\mathbf{n}_i$ and a discretisation of size $h_e$. Here we consider the opening and sliding modes for the fracture, as shown in Fig. A.13b and c, where the crack purely undergoes deformation corresponding to the opening and sliding modes, respectively. In the former, i.e., the opening mode, the aperture is equal to the elongation of the element normal to the crack direction. While in the latter case, it is evident that only the shear strain is involved without inducing any crack opening. In the opening mode, the crack aperture can be obtained by multiplication of the vertical strain, calculated at the Gauss integration points, by the element size. To generalise this approach to cases of horizontal or vertical cracks, where either $\epsilon_x$ or $\epsilon_y$ has a non-zero value, this relationship can be expressed as the product of the volumetric strain and the element size, where $\epsilon_{\mathrm{vol}} = \epsilon_x + \epsilon_y$. Thus, the crack opening can be described by Eq. (38).

A more general scenario where the crack can have any orientation relative to the element is depicted in Fig. A.14a. Here, as illustrated in Fig. A.14b, the element experiences strains in both directions. Neglecting the shear strain corresponding to the sliding mode, the element undergoes strains $\epsilon_x$ and $\epsilon_y$. The horizontal and vertical elongations can be obtained as a multiplication of $\epsilon_x$, $\epsilon_y$ and the element size, respectively. As explained in Section 2.2, the crack opening perpendicular to the fracture is employed to calculate the permeability of the fracture based on the cubic law. The crack aperture, being the opening perpendicular to the fracture orientation, should take into account both horizontal and vertical openings. Hence, the dot product of $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n = (\epsilon_x, \epsilon_y)$ and $\mathbf{n}_i$ results in the strain normal to the fracture which can result in the crack opening value once multiplied by the element size. The COD can be expressed by Eq. (35), where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_n$ is a 2-by-1 vector containing $\epsilon_x$ and $\epsilon_y$ for the two-dimensional case. It is noteworthy that the application considered in this study (i.e., hydraulic fracturing treatments) is primarily dominated by the opening mode.
