## Abstract

We present an enriched radial basis function (RBF) interpolation, which is combined with a symmetric Galerkin method to solve finite strain problems. Specifically, Wendland compact support radial basis functions (RBF) are enriched with a quadratic polynomial, whose parameters are condensed-out during the shape-function construction phase. A total Lagrangian technique is adopted and for finite strain plasticity the formalism with the Mandel stress is adopted. These recent developments are especially convenient from the implementation perspective, as RBF formulations for finite strain plasticity have been limited by interpolation updating. We also note that, although tetrahedra are adopted for quadrature in the undeformed configuration, mesh deformation is of no consequence for the results. Four benchmark tests are successfully solved, with extensive convergence studies and showing clear advantages with respect to finite-element in terms of robustness and with respect to classical Element-Free Galerkin (EFG) formulations in terms of efficiency.

## 1. Introduction

Realism in simulations of engineering materials depends on the quality of finite strain analyses. In finite strain plasticity, yield functions depend on stress values and these are, in traditional FE methods, discontinuous between elements. Compared with displacements, errors in stresses are a magnitude higher, even without accounting for constitutive constraints such as incompressibility. Quadratic and cubic finite elements are not frequently adopted in finite strain elasto-plastic analysis due the following shortcomings:

- Mesh distortion in high order elements has a severe impact on convergence rate [1]. Adaptive remeshing is required more often with high-order elements. Even with a total Lagrangian formulation, mesh distortion requires changes to the finite element mesh, since the spatial Jacobian is required for the calculation of the deformation gradient.
- High-order problems require dedicated techniques or architecture-intensive isogeometric formulations [2]. Contact algorithms are often more intricate for higher-order elements. Classical meshless methods based on least-squares formulations also involve some intricacies.

Although stress quality improves with the degree of the complete polynomial, in finite element methods, discontinuities are still present at inter-element boundaries [3]. Plasticity results are dependent on the quality of the stresses, which is compromised in finite elements. Although EFG [4,5] formulations have been shown to solve many of these shortcomings, recent developments with enriched radial basis functions provide a more definitive solution to these problems, see [6]. A comprehensive presentation of developments in meshless methods (including RBF) was published by J.-S. Chen and co-workers [7].

The RBF method as part of the solution PDE was introduced by Kansa in 1990 [8], although at that time, multiquadrics have been explored as interpolators for two decades. Compact-support RBF interpolations are described in detail in the book by Buhmann [9], where an upper bound on the error is derived. These compact-support functions were established by Wu [10] and Wendland [11]. A related formulation is the (L)RPM method by G.R. Liu and co-workers [12], which combines the point interpolation method (PIM) with radial basis functions (RBF) and that has been explored with success by other groups [13]. Two properties of RBF are very attractive for Engineering:

1. the satisfaction of the Kronecker-Delta property regardless of the number of functions used and
2. strict positive definite interpolation matrix, also proved in the presence of enrichment in [9].

Recently, the analysis of [6] showed that RBF enriched with polynomials are more robust than classical polynomial-based MLS.

We note that in RPM, globally supported radially-decreasing RBF are adopted, which are truncated to impose a compact support [14]. In this work, we make use of strictly compact-support RBF functions.

Along parallel lines of research, Atluri's MLPG (meshless local Petrov-Galerkin) method [15,16] established a general case for Galerkin-based meshless methods based on distinct trial and test functions. In the context of RBF combined with MLPG, trial functions are typically enriched RBF terms combined with lower-order test functions, often step functions [17,18]. We note that in most Engineering-relevant papers, a local, or compact version of both trial and test functions is used [11,18].

Hu et al. [17] have developed Atluri's group MLPG method [15,16] for hyperelasticity and contact. However, the use of enriched RBF functions with a classical (Bubnov) Galerkin method is still relatively unexplored in finite strain analysis. A related approach combining RBF and the reproducing kernel particle method (RKPM) was introduced recently by Liu et al. [19] for small strain plasticity of functionally graded materials.

Compared with recent developments, our approach is based on a mesh and finite-element positions of quadrature points, with support being predetermined by each element centroid. We use strictly compact RBF enriched with a quadratic polynomial. In contrast with classical developments in finite-strain plasticity with meshless methods (e.g. [20]), we adopt a total Lagrangian formulation, made possible by our constitutive framework based on the iteration for $\mathbf{C}_e$ [21]. In contrast, hyperelastic implementations of meshless methods are common, and recent papers report realistic results with high degree of continuity, see [5] for a Galerkin-based approach and Hosseini et al. [22] for a collocation RBF algorithm in hyperelasticity. A different RBF-based Galerkin algorithm was introduced by Ho and [23] where the second derivative of the shape-functions is interpolated with RBF and then an integration process follows.

The practical advantages of a total Lagrangian formulation are more significant in meshless methods than in finite element analysis. Unlike finite element formulations for finite strains, no transformation of coordinates is adopted, there is no need for a deformed-configuration Jacobian matrix. Note that in standard finite element analysis, the deformation gradient $\mathbf{F}$ is determined by the chain rule $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{j} \cdot \mathbf{J}^{-1}$ where $\mathbf{j}$ is the deformed-configuration Jacobian matrix and $\mathbf{J}$ is the undeformed-configuration Jacobian matrix [24]. This means that even if $\det \mathbf{J} > 0$, if $\det \mathbf{j} \leq 0$ a new mesh is required to continue the analysis.

This work is organized as follows: Section 2 presents the interpolation, as well as the algorithm to guarantee a sufficiently small support radius. Section 3 presents the discretization based on the total Lagrangian approach, followed by Section 4 where the constitutive integration, conforming to the developments of Section 3, is described in detail. In Section 5 four benchmark tests are performed, including convergence studies, and finally conclusions are drawn in Section 6.

## 2. General format of interpolation

### 2.1. Enriched radial basis functions (RBF)

Classical radial basis function derivations can be consulted in Bayona [6] and in Buhmann [9]. Given $n_a$ nodes belonging to the support set $\Omega_a$, an interpolative function $w(\mathbf{X})$ can be manufactured by multiplying a set of $n_a$ radial basis functions $A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X})]$ by a set of $n_a$ unknown parameters $a_{i_a}$, with $i_a = 1,\ldots,n_a$:

$$
w(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})] \cdot \mathbf{a} = \sum_{i_a=1}^{n_a} A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X})] a_{i_a} \tag{1}
$$

where $r(\mathbf{X})$ is the Euclidean distance between $\mathbf{X}_{i_a}$ and $\mathbf{X}$:

$$
r(\mathbf{X}) = \|\mathbf{X}_{i_a} - \mathbf{X}\|_2 \tag{2}
$$

The interpolation condition for node $N$ is written by specializing the inner product (1):

$$
w_N = w(\mathbf{X}_N) = \sum_{i_a=1}^{n_a} A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X}_N)] a_{i_a} \tag{3}
$$

We now introduce the notation $\mathbf{A}_{N i_a} = A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X}_N)]$ and $\mathbf{w} = \{w_1,\ldots,w_{n_a}\}^T$, which results in the following solution for $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{w} \tag{4}
$$

Inserting $\mathbf{a}$ in the interpolation formula (1), we obtain the inner product of a shape function array and the nodal images of the function:

$$
w(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{X}) \cdot \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})] \cdot \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{w} \tag{5}
$$

where $\mathbf{N}(\mathbf{X}) = \{N_1(\mathbf{X}),\ldots,N_{n_a}(\mathbf{X})\}^T$ is the shape function array. Form (5) shares most properties with the corresponding finite-element notion of shape functions. If a monomial basis term $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X})$ of dimension $n_b$ is appended to the interpolation, we obtain:

$$
w(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})] \cdot \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X}) \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i_a=1}^{n_a} A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X})] a_{i_a} + \sum_{i_b=1}^{n_b} B_{i_b}(\mathbf{X}) b_{i_b} \tag{6}
$$

where $\{b_1,\ldots,b_{n_b}\}$ are additional interpolation parameters. The nodal evaluation of $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X})$ is similar to the previous notation:

$$
\mathbf{B}_{N i_a} = B_{i_a}(\mathbf{X}_N) \tag{7}
$$

If $\mathbf{w}$ are the images of a given polynomial $p^\star(\mathbf{X})$ evaluated at nodes $\mathbf{X}_{i_a}$ with $i_a = 1,\ldots,n_a$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{\rho}^\star = \{p^\star(\mathbf{X}_1),\ldots,p^\star(\mathbf{X}_{n_a})\}^T$, then

$$
\mathbf{w} = \boldsymbol{\rho}^\star \tag{8}
$$

For this polynomial $p^\star(\mathbf{X})$, we have the result for $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot (\mathbf{w} - \boldsymbol{\rho}^\star) = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot (\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{b}) \tag{9}
$$

We now pre-multiply both sides of (9) by $\mathbf{B}^T$ this will result in

$$
\mathbf{B}^T \cdot \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0} \tag{10}
$$

This condition is the polynomial reproducing condition, see [6]. The full interpolation system is given by:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \\
\mathbf{B}^T & \mathbf{0}
\end{bmatrix} \cdot
\begin{Bmatrix}
\mathbf{a} \\
\mathbf{b}
\end{Bmatrix} =
\begin{Bmatrix}
\mathbf{w} \\
\mathbf{0}
\end{Bmatrix} \tag{11}
$$

In contrast with applied-mathematics derivations of system (11), the purpose here is to ensure the reproduction of constant and linear displacement fields, the so-called patch test [25]. For non-singular $\mathbf{A}$ we have the classical solution for $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{a} &= \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{H} \right] \cdot \mathbf{w} \tag{12} \\
\mathbf{b} &= \mathbf{H} \cdot \mathbf{w} \tag{13}
\end{aligned}
$$

where

$$
\mathbf{H} = \left( \mathbf{B}^T \cdot \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{B} \right)^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{B}^T \cdot \mathbf{A}^{-1} \tag{14}
$$

is obtained for non-singular $\mathbf{A}$. We note that the matrix $\left( \mathbf{B}^T \cdot \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{B} \right)$ in (14) is at most $10 \times 10$ for a quadratic monomial in 3D and a dedicated subroutine can be adopted. With these results, we obtain the following form for the shape functions:

$$
\mathbf{N}(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})] \cdot \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X}) \cdot \mathbf{H} \tag{15}
$$

with

$$
\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \cdot \left[ \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{H} \right] \tag{16}
$$

This also ensures the non-singularity of the interpolation problem, see Chapter 5 of [9]. The polynomial reproduction property ensures a convergence order determined by the degree of polynomial basis, see [6].

In contrast with the MLS-based polynomial approximation [4] or quasi-interpolation, shape function derivatives do not involve the calculation of the derivative of the inverse matrix. For reasons of conciseness, the component-wise format is presented here for both the first and second derivatives:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i} &= \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})]}{\partial r} \cdot \mathbf{G} \frac{\partial r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i} \cdot \mathbf{H} \tag{17} \\
\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i \partial X_j} &= \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})]}{\partial r^2} \cdot \mathbf{G} \frac{\partial r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i} \frac{\partial r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_j} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})]}{\partial r} \cdot \mathbf{G} \frac{\partial^2 r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i \partial X_j} + \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i \partial X_j} \cdot \mathbf{H} \tag{18}
\end{aligned}
$$

with

$$
\frac{\partial r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i} = \frac{1}{r(\mathbf{X})} \left[ X_i - X_i^{i_a} \right] \tag{19}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial^2 r(\mathbf{X})}{\partial X_i \partial X_j} = \frac{1}{r(\mathbf{X})} \delta_{ij} - \frac{1}{[r(\mathbf{X})]^3} \left[ X_i - X_i^{i_a} \right] \left[ X_j - X_j^{i_a} \right] \tag{20}
$$

where $i_a$ is implicitly identified by the radius function $r(\mathbf{X})$.

### 2.2. Specialization

We now specialize the basis $\mathbf{A}[r(\mathbf{X})]$ and $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X})$ as a radial basis functions basis and a quadratic basis, respectively. Compact-support RBF basis by Wendland are used, see [11,9]. These are the appropriate forms for solid mechanics, where the quadratic case in 3D is written:

$$
A_{i_a}[r(\mathbf{X})] =
\begin{cases}
(1 - r/r_{\max})^4_+ (1 + 4r/r_{\max}) & r \leq r_{\max} \\
0 & r > r_{\max}
\end{cases} \tag{21}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{X}) = \{1, X_1, X_2, X_3, X_1 X_2, X_1 X_3, X_2 X_3, X_1^2, X_2^2, X_3^2\} \tag{22}
$$

Implementation is based on the following steps, performed at the start of the analysis:

1. For each face $F$ of the tetrahedra mesh, flag if it is a boundary face. Each boundary face shares only one tetrahedron.
2. For each node $K$, flag it as boundary node if it shares a boundary face.
3. Loop over all nodes that are not at the boundary:
   (a) For each non-boundary node $K$ determine the smallest edge containing $K$.
   (b) Sets $r_L$ as $10\%$ of its length as the maximum perturbation.
   (c) Using a sphere of radius $r_L$ and center $K$ randomly determines the new position of node $K$ inside the sphere.
4. Loop over all tetrahedra. For tetrahedron $E$:
   (a) For a given list of size $n \geq 4$, insert:
       i. All nodes of the tetrahedron.
       ii. Other $n-4$ closest nodes to the centroid of $E$.
   (b) Use the farthest node in the list to determine the support radius $D$ for tetrahedron $E$.
   (c) Use Eqs. (17) to calculate the shape function derivatives.
5. In the Newton-Raphson iteration loop, only $\mathbf{C}$ is calculated, shape function derivatives are stored in memory.

In terms of quadrature, we use a finite-element mesh with triangles and tetrahedra, where the only FE-related calculations are the area and the traditional Gauss-Legendre weights. A given node $K$ belonging to the elementwise estimate is within the support of a quadrature point if

$$
\|\mathbf{X}_K - \mathbf{X}_G\| \leq r_{\max}^C - \|\mathbf{X}_G - \mathbf{X}_C\|
$$

where $\mathbf{X}_K$ are the (undeformed) coordinates of node $K$, $\mathbf{X}_G$ are the coordinates of the quadrature point and $\mathbf{X}_C$ are the centroid coordinates of the underlying triangle or tetrahedron. The parameter $r_{\max}^C$, which is the radius of elementwise support is determined by pre-assignment of the number of support nodes by the inverse relation:

$$
N_C = \# \left\{ K \in \mathcal{N} \mid \|\mathbf{X}_K - \mathbf{X}_C\| \leq r_{\max}^C \right\}
$$

where $\mathcal{N}$ is the total number of nodes. We sort the nodes according to their distance to $C$ and $r_{\max}^C$ is the distance of the $N_C$-th closest node to $C$. Fig. 1 shows the support domains for the quadrature points.

## 3. Discretized equilibrium equations

A straightforward total Lagrangian implementation is followed (see, e.g. [24]). We make use of the definition of the right Cauchy-Green tensor:

$$
\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{X}_h) = \mathbf{F}^T(\mathbf{X}_h) \cdot \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{X}_h) \tag{23}
$$

For a given point $\mathbf{X}_h$ with discrete support $\Omega_{\mathbf{X}_h}$, we have:

$$
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{X}_h) = \frac{d\mathbf{x}_h}{d\mathbf{X}_h} = \sum_{L \in \Omega_{\mathbf{X}_h}} \left( \frac{d N_L(\mathbf{X}_h) \mathbf{x}_L}{d\mathbf{X}_h} \right) \tag{24}
$$

Or, in terms of components and omitting the dependence on $\mathbf{X}_h$, we obtain the components of $\mathbf{F}$ as:

$$
F_{ij} = \frac{d N_L}{d X_j} x_{iL} \tag{25}
$$

Using the variation symbol $\delta$ we introduce the variation of $\mathbf{F}$, in the equilibrium sense, as:

$$
\delta F_{ij} = \frac{d N_L}{d X_j} \delta x_{iL} \tag{26}
$$

Introducing the notation $N_{jL} = d N_L / d X_j$ for the shape-function derivatives, the following results for $\mathbf{C}$ and its first and second variations are obtained:

$$
\begin{aligned}
C_{ij} &= N_{iK} N_{jL} x_{kK} x_{kL} \quad \Rightarrow \\
\delta C_{ij} &= N_{iK} N_{jL} (x_{kL} \delta x_{kK} + x_{kK} \delta x_{kL}) \\
\dot{C}_{ij} &= N_{iK} N_{jL} (x_{kL} \dot{x}_{kK} + x_{kK} \dot{x}_{kL}) \\
\delta \dot{C}_{ij} &= N_{iK} N_{jL} (\dot{x}_{kL} \delta x_{kK} + \dot{x}_{kK} \delta x_{kL})
\end{aligned}
$$

Equilibrium is established in weak form by the use of the second Piola–Kirchhoff stress $\mathbf{S}$ and the spatial configuration variation $\delta \mathbf{x}$:

$$
\frac{1}{2} \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{S} : \delta \mathbf{C} \, d\Omega_0 = \mathbf{f}^{\text{ext}} \cdot \delta \mathbf{x} \tag{27}
$$

For the application of Newton–Raphson iteration, we require the first variation of (27). To avoid confusion with the variation symbol $\delta$, we use the time-derivative to denote the variation of equilibrium. By taking this time-derivative variation, the tangent modulus $\mathcal{C}$ is employed to read:

$$
\frac{1}{2} \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{S} : \delta \dot{\mathbf{C}} \, d\Omega_0 + \frac{1}{4} \int_{\Omega_0} \delta \mathbf{C} : \mathcal{C} : \dot{\mathbf{C}} \, d\Omega_0 = \mathbf{f}^{\text{ext}} \cdot \delta \dot{\mathbf{x}} - \frac{1}{2} \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{S} : \delta \mathbf{C} \, d\Omega_0 \tag{28}
$$

where $\mathbf{f}^{\text{ext}}$ is the external load vector and $\dot{\mathbf{x}}$ is the nodal velocity vector. Note that, in the implementation, the second derivative of $\mathbf{C}$ is required in $\delta \dot{\mathbf{C}}$. In Voigt form (see [26]), we have the following internal force and tangent stiffness:

$$
\mathbf{f}_L = \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{B}_L^T \cdot \hat{\mathbf{S}} \, d\Omega_0 \tag{29}
$$

$$
\mathbf{K}_{KL} = \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{B}_K^T \cdot \mathcal{C} \cdot \mathbf{B}_L \, d\Omega_0 + \int_{\Omega_0} \mathbf{B}_K^{\star T} \cdot \check{\mathbf{S}} \cdot \mathbf{B}_L^\star \, d\Omega_0 \tag{30}
$$

Omitting dependence on $\mathbf{X}$, notation in (29)–(30) is as follows:

$$
\mathbf{B}_L =
\begin{bmatrix}
F_{11} N_{1L} \\
F_{21} N_{1L} \\
F_{31} N_{1L} \\
F_{12} N_{2L} \\
F_{22} N_{2L} \\
F_{32} N_{2L} \\
F_{13} N_{3L} \\
F_{23} N_{3L} \\
F_{33} N_{3L} \\
F_{11} N_{2L} + F_{12} N_{1L} \\
F_{21} N_{2L} + F_{22} N_{1L} \\
F_{31} N_{2L} + F_{32} N_{1L} \\
F_{13} N_{1L} + F_{11} N_{3L} \\
F_{23} N_{1L} + F_{21} N_{3L} \\
F_{33} N_{1L} + F_{31} N_{3L} \\
F_{12} N_{3L} + F_{13} N_{2L} \\
F_{22} N_{3L} + F_{23} N_{2L} \\
F_{32} N_{3L} + F_{33} N_{2L}
\end{bmatrix} \tag{31}
$$

A specific form of $\mathbf{B}_K^\star$ in (30) is not needed at this point, but can be consulted in [3]. The Voigt form of the second Piola–Kirchhoff stress is given by:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{S}} =
\begin{Bmatrix}
S_{11} \\
S_{22} \\
S_{33} \\
S_{12} \\
S_{13} \\
S_{23}
\end{Bmatrix} \tag{32}
$$

We also have:

$$
\mathbf{B}_K^T \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{S}} \cdot \mathbf{B}_L^* = r_{KL} \mathbf{I}_{3\times 3} \tag{33}
$$

where

$$
r_{KL} =
\begin{Bmatrix} N_{1K} \\ N_{2K} \\ N_{3K} \end{Bmatrix}^T \cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
S_{11} & S_{12} & S_{13} \\
S_{12} & S_{22} & S_{23} \\
S_{13} & S_{23} & S_{33}
\end{bmatrix} \cdot
\begin{Bmatrix} N_{1L} \\ N_{2L} \\ N_{3L} \end{Bmatrix} \tag{34}
$$

which is a compact form for the geometric stiffness. In contrast with advanced finite element formulations [27,28], these are classical and direct derivations. In addition, shape functions and corresponding derivatives are calculated once in the beginning of the solution process.

## 4. Hyperelasticity/plasticity using the elastic mandel stress tensor

### 4.1. Formulation

The Mandel stress tensor approach to finite-strain plasticity is adopted [29,30]. We make use of the Kroner-Lee decomposition [31,32] (see also [33]):

$$
\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_e \cdot \mathbf{F}_p \tag{35}
$$

Using (35), the velocity gradient is determined by its definition and then partitioned as follows:

$$
\mathbf{L} = \dot{\mathbf{F}} \cdot \mathbf{F}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}_e + \mathbf{F}_e \cdot \mathbf{L}_p \cdot \mathbf{F}_e^{-1} \tag{36}
$$

with $\mathbf{L}_e = \dot{\mathbf{F}}_e \cdot \mathbf{F}_e^{-1}$, the elastic velocity gradient and $\mathbf{L}_p = \dot{\mathbf{F}}_p \cdot \mathbf{F}_p^{-1}$, the plastic velocity gradient. The second Piola-Kirchhoff stress is a function of the elastic part of $\mathbf{F}$ by means of $\mathbf{C}_e = \mathbf{F}_e^T \cdot \mathbf{F}_e$ (cf. [34] page 166), the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress at the intermediate configuration is given by $\mathbf{S}_e(\mathbf{C}_e)$ (see [35]), from which energy consistency results in a second Piola-Kirchhoff stress $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{F}_p^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{S}_e(\mathbf{C}_e) \cdot \mathbf{F}_p^{-T}$. In the hyperelastic case, a strain energy density function $\psi(\mathbf{C}_e)$ exists such as

$$
\mathbf{S}_e(\mathbf{C}_e) = 2 \frac{d\psi(\mathbf{C}_e)}{d\mathbf{C}_e} \tag{37}
$$

The compressible Neo-Hookean model is also used, with the following strain-energy density function:

$$
\psi(\mathbf{C}_e) = \frac{\mu}{2} [\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C}_e) - 3] - \mu \log \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{C}_e)} + \frac{\lambda}{2} \left[ \log \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{C}_e)} \right]^2 \tag{38}
$$

The flow law follows similar arguments [30], assuming that the initial plastic deformation gradient corresponds to the identity, $[\mathbf{F}_p]_0 = \mathbf{I}$. Agreeing with standard derivations on plasticity, a yield function $\phi$ is introduced, as well as a plastic multiplier $\dot{\gamma}$. Introducing the notation $\mathbf{Q}_p = \mathbf{F}_p^{-1}$. We summarize the constitutive system as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathbf{S} = \mathbf{Q}_p \cdot \mathbf{S}_e(\mathbf{C}_e) \cdot \mathbf{Q}_p^T \tag{39} \\
& \dot{\mathbf{Q}}_p = -\dot{\gamma} \mathbf{Q}_p \cdot \mathbf{N}[\mathbf{T}_e] \tag{40} \\
& [\mathbf{Q}_p]_0 = \mathbf{I} \tag{41} \\
& \langle \phi(\mathbf{T}_e) + \dot{\gamma} \rangle - \dot{\gamma} = 0 \tag{42}
\end{aligned}
$$

with $\langle \bullet \rangle = \frac{\bullet + |\bullet|}{2}$ being the unit ramp function. In (40), the Mandel stress [29] $\mathbf{T}_e$ is given by:

$$
\mathbf{T}_e = \mathbf{C}_e \cdot \mathbf{S}_e(\mathbf{C}_e) \tag{43}
$$

Assuming an associated flow law [33], we have the flow vector $\mathbf{N}(\mathbf{T}_e)$ determined from the derivative of $\phi(\mathbf{T}_e)$:

$$
\mathbf{N}(\mathbf{T}_e) = \frac{d\phi(\mathbf{T}_e)}{d\mathbf{T}_e} \tag{44}
$$

Power equivalence provides the effective plastic strain rate $\dot{\epsilon}_p$ as a function of the yield stress $\sigma_y$:

$$
\dot{\epsilon}_p = \dot{\gamma} \frac{\mathbf{T}_e : \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{T}_e)}{\sigma_y} \tag{45}
$$

### 4.2. Constitutive integration

For the constitutive integration, we use superscripts $n$ and $n+1$ to identify two consecutive time-steps and $\Delta t$ as the time-step size. Applying the backward-Euler method for $\dot{\mathbf{Q}}_p$ and $\dot{\gamma}$ results in:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathbf{Q}_p^{n+1} = \mathbf{Q}_p^n \cdot \left[ \mathbf{I} + \Delta \gamma \widehat{\mathbf{N}}(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}) \right]^{-1} = \left[ \Delta \widehat{\mathbf{Q}}(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma) \right]^{-1} \tag{46} \\
& \gamma^{n+1} = \gamma^n + \underbrace{\dot{\gamma}^{n+1} \Delta t}_{\Delta \gamma} \tag{47}
\end{aligned}
$$

We now define the elastic trial Cauchy-Green tensor as $\mathbf{C}_e^\star = [\mathbf{Q}_p^n]^T \cdot \mathbf{C}^{n+1} \cdot \mathbf{Q}_p^n$. Introducing the function $\widehat{C}_e^\star(\mathbf{C}^{n+1}) = (\mathbf{Q}_p^n)^T \cdot \mathbf{C}^{n+1} \cdot \mathbf{Q}_p^n$, the constitutive system for $\Delta \gamma > 0$ consists of the following equations:

$$
\left[ \Delta \widehat{\mathbf{Q}}(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma) \right]^T \cdot \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} \cdot \left[ \Delta \widehat{\mathbf{Q}}(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma) \right] - \widehat{C}_e^\star(\mathbf{C}^{n+1}) = \mathbf{0} \tag{48}
$$

$$
\mathbf{r}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma, \mathbf{C}^{n+1}) \quad \phi_\star \left[ \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{S}}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}) \right] = 0 \tag{49}
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}$ is symmetric, Voigt notation can be used, $\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} = \text{Voigt}[\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}]$ and $\mathbf{r}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma, \mathbf{C}^{n+1}) = \text{Voigt}[\mathbf{r}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma, \mathbf{C}^{n+1})]$. Omitting the function arguments for conciseness, the Newton-Raphson iteration for $\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}$ (Voigt form) and $\Delta \gamma$ is written as:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_c}{\partial \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}} & \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_c}{\partial \Delta \gamma} \\
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}} & 0
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\Delta \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} \\
\Delta \Delta \gamma
\end{bmatrix} = -
\begin{Bmatrix}
\mathbf{r}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}, \Delta \gamma, \mathbf{C}^{n+1}) \\
\phi_\star \left[ \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{S}}_c(\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}) \right]
\end{Bmatrix} \tag{50}
$$

with $\mathbf{Y} = \{\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1} \ \Delta \gamma\}^T$ being the constitutive unknowns for this problem. Following $\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}$, $\mathbf{Q}_p^{n+1}$ is determined by (46) and the second Piola-Kirchhoff stress at step $n+1$ is calculated from its definition. Stress sensitivity, the determination of the consistent modulus, with $\mathbf{S}^{n+1} = \text{Voigt}[\mathbf{S}^{n+1}]$, is determined as follows:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{S}^{n+1}}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} = \frac{\partial \widehat{\mathbf{S}}^{n+1}}{\partial \mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{C}_e^{n+1}}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} + \frac{\partial \widehat{\mathbf{S}}^{n+1}}{\partial \Delta \gamma} \frac{d\Delta \gamma}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} \tag{51}
$$

In (51), a single dot-product is adopted for double contraction of quantities in Voigt form. From (51) we can conclude that $\mathcal{C}$ is determined as a function of the solution of (50), since

$$
\frac{d\Delta \gamma}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} = -\mathbf{J}^{-1} \cdot \frac{d\gamma}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} \tag{52}
$$

therefore, stress sensitivity is simply given by:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{S}^{n+1}}{d\mathbf{C}^{n+1}} = -\left( d\widehat{\mathbf{S}}^{n+1} / d\mathbf{Y} \right) \cdot \left( d\mathbf{Y} / d\mathbf{C}^{n+1} \right) \tag{53}
$$

The effective plastic strain rate follows the integration of (45):

$$
\epsilon_p^{n+1} = \epsilon_p^n + \Delta \gamma \frac{\mathbf{T}_e : \mathbf{N}(\mathbf{T}_e)}{\sigma_y} \tag{54}
$$

The nondimensional yield function is given by:

$$
\phi_\star(\mathbf{T}_e) = \frac{\sigma_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{T}_e)}{\sigma_y} - 1 \tag{55}
$$

where, as a prototype equivalent stress, a specific Hill48 criterion (1948 [36]) is adopted. The general form of the Hill48 equivalent stress $\sigma_{\text{eq}}$ is written as:

$$
\sigma_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{T}_e) = \sqrt{ F(T_{22} - T_{33})^2 + G(T_{33} - T_{11})^2 + H(T_{11} - T_{22})^2 + 2S_1(T_4^s)^2 + 2S_2(T_5^s)^2 + 2S_3(T_6^s)^2 } \tag{56}
$$

Two cases of Hill48 yield function are herein used:

1. von-Mises, $\mathbf{y} = \{1,1,1,1,1,1\}$
2. DP780 steel as measured by Zhu et al. [37], $\mathbf{y} = \{1,0.9543,0.8757,0.8213,0.8213,0.8213\}$

### 5.1. Straight cantilever beam with closed-form solution

We start with the Timoshenko and Goodier [42] cantilever beam in small-strain elasticity for two values of the Poisson coefficient: $\nu = 0.3$ and $\nu = 0.49999$. The quasi-incompressible case is here specified with a plane-strain assumption. A comparison with element-free Galerkin (EFG) and the MINI element by D. Arnold [43] is performed. The beam is represented in Fig. 2.

We use the first slope boundary condition by Timoshenko and Goodier who obtained the solution for the displacement in the plane stress case. Introducing this displacement solution into the strain components and making use of Hooke's law, we calculate the strain energy per unit thickness as follows:

$$
U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^l \left[ \int_{-c}^{+c} \left( \epsilon_{xx} \sigma_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} \sigma_{yy} + \gamma_{xy} \tau_{xy} \right) dy \right] dx \tag{58}
$$

The plane strain case is obtained replacing $E$ by $E/(1-\nu^2)$ and $\nu$ by $\nu/(1-\nu)$. Strain energy per unit thickness is given, for the plane strain and plane stress cases, by:

$$
U_{\text{plane strain}} = \frac{P^2 \left[ 6c^2 E l + 5 G l^3 (1-\nu^2) \right]}{20 c^3 G E}, \quad
U_{\text{plane stress}} = \frac{P^2 (6c^2 E l + 5 G l^3)}{20 c^3 G E}
$$

### 5.2. Linear and nonlinear versions of Cook's membrane

We revisit Cook's membrane [44] problem in three cases: (i) linear elastic case, (ii) quasi-incompressible hyperelasticity and (iii) finite-strain plasticity. It consists of a trapezoidal cantilever beam loaded uniformly in its smaller edge. It combines bending and shearing effects and has been a staple of discretization benchmarking, see [45], and it is important as a comparison between element technology, EFG and the present RBF formulation. (See Fig. 5.)

We found that, in contrast with RBF, where only one quadrature point is sufficient, to avoid the lack of stiffness in the EFG case, 6 quadrature points per triangle are required. In addition, RBF can make use of small supports, with fewer than 20 nodes in 2D. Results from EFG start to deteriorate for smaller supports and the least-squares system becomes singular for fewer than 30 nodes. The effect is shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. 6.

For the finite-strain case, two constitutive laws are used: quasi-incompressible Neo-Hookean elasticity and $J_2$ elasto-plasticity, as described by Simo and Armero [46]. Fig. 7 shows the relevant data and contour plots for $\epsilon_p$ and $\sigma_H = \operatorname{tr} \mathbf{S} / 3$ in the elasto-plastic case. For the coarse distribution of nodes, relatively smooth contour plots for these quantities are obtained, benefiting the elasto-plastic analysis. In Fig. 8, we present the load factor vs tip displacement results for the T3 element, EFG and RBF formulations. We can observe that oscillations occur with the T3 element, even with a crossed-triangle mesh arrangement. This is caused by jumps between load steps with discontinuous stresses. Neither EFG nor RBF exhibit this behavior.

For the quasi-incompressible neo-Hookean case (with a milder $\nu = 0.4999$) we can observe the tip displacement convergence behavior for T3, EFG and RBF formulations. The reference solution was obtained with a $300 \times 300$ partition (RBF, quadratic). The combination of RBF with a quadratic polynomial clearly outperforms the other formulations, see Fig. 9.

Using the DP780 steel properties (adapted from Zhu et al. [37]), $\mathbf{y} = \{1, 0.9543, 0.8757, 0.8213, 0.8213, 0.8213\}$, the convergence study is shown in Fig. 10. A noticeable non-monotonous EFG behavior can be observed, and this is caused by the use of one point integration, that we retain since it causes no issues with the RBF formulations.

### 5.3. Block compression

Using a quasi-incompressible Neo-Hookean law, we assess the present finite-strain RBF formulation with a benchmark consisting of an asymmetric compression of a block as described by Reese, Wriggers and Reddy [47].

$$

Therefore, this is a demanding test to assess the effect of the polynomial basis. Fig. 11 shows the relevant data for this problem. In the three cases, very large deformations are reached without indication of instabilities. The contour plot of stress component $S_{22}$ is smooth, even in the highly deformed region, confirming the effectiveness of the RBF formulation. For comparison, we show the T4 and the EFG results.

In Fig. 13 results for $N_C = 20$ with linear and quadratic basis are presented. For comparison, we also report the results by Caylak and Mahnken [48], using the reduced integration hexahedron Q1R and 8 elements per edge, and a classical tetrahedron T4 with a crossed-configuration (i.e. a hexahedral mesh with a regular division in 24 tetrahedra). As can be observed, results with lower values of $N_C$ converge to the Caylak and Mahnken solution, even in the coarser case. Finer nodal distributions produce more flexible results and with the quadratic basis results are nearly insensitive to the nodal refinement. The effect of $N_C$ is reported in Fig. 13(c), which confirms our previous conclusions. Clearly, results for $N_C = 20$ and $N_C = 30$ are very close in terms of results.

### 5.4. Upsetting test

We make use of M.A. Puso and J. Solberg upsetting test [49] in its two elasto-plastic versions (respectively linear and power hardening). Geometry, boundary conditions and constitutive properties for this test are shown in Fig. 14. Two uniform meshes are adopted for comparison, containing 527 and 903 nodes. Nodes are forced to remain above a horizontal plane by a non-penetration condition. The elasto-plastic case described by M. Puso is the most demanding and it is reported that only their nodal-integrated and stabilized UT4s provides acceptable results from the stability and accuracy standpoints. We point out that, in [49], a stabilization parameter is required. In the present formulation, no parameters are required and no hourglass instabilities were detected. However, we detected some convergence difficulties for large values of the vertical displacement when $N_C < 15$. Fig. 15 shows the very smooth contour plots for $\epsilon_p$ and hydrostatic stress $\sigma_H$. Fig. 16(a) presents a comparison between nodal distributions, support sizes ($N_C$) and basis dimension. All three factors contribute to a more flexible behavior: finer meshes are less stiff, larger supports produce softer behavior and quadratic basis produce results beneath the reaction–displacement curve reported in [49]. EFG results are again exceedingly flexible, confirming our previous observations.

## 6. Conclusions

We introduced a triangle/tetrahedra-based RBF formulation enriched with a quadratic polynomial basis. The following properties are set:

- Polynomial parameters are condensed-out during shape function construction.
- A pre-assignment of support nodes is based on the integration cells, i.e. the triangles and tetrahedra. During quadrature, some nodes outside the quadrature domain are discarded.
- Constitutive integration makes use of the Mandel stress tensor and iteration on $\mathbf{C}_e$ [21].

Some conclusions are drawn with respect to the least-squares EFG formulation:

- Shape function derivatives are very cost-effective, since no requirement exists for the derivative of an inverse matrix.
- The interpolation problem is always well-posed, with a positive-definite interpolation matrix.
- The Kronecker-Delta property is always satisfied by the combination of RBF and polynomial enrichment. In EFG, special weight functions must be adopted to approximately satisfy this property.
- A polynomial of any degree can be used as an enrichment, since no significant computational cost exists, due to condensation.

Implementation is straightforward and was performed in SimPlas [38] with AceGen [40] and Mathematica [39]. Four benchmark tests were performed with extensive convergence studies, which allow the following conclusions:

Compared with EFG, smaller values of $N_C$ are possible without significant decay of results. For a given fixed number of nodes, quasi-incompressible results were significantly better than those obtained with EFG or crossed-triangle/tetrahedra finite elements.

The number of support nodes, $N_C$, is much less determinant in RBF than with classical EFG, as the Cook's membrane shows. This was found to be an important argument in favor of robustness of simulations. Even with small values of $N_C$, results are highly competitive with established finite elements. The quadratic basis exhibited excellent results when compared with Finite Elements, EFG and also the linear basis version of RBF. This conclusion is aligned with the theoretical analysis of Bayona [6]. One-point quadrature was found to be sufficient for all examples, which is convenient for costly constitutive formulations.
